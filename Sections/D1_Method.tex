\section{Method}
In optimising the properties of a poly(glycerol citrate itaconate) thermoset, 
 interlinking method was developed spanning both practical experimental technique
 and mathematical framing.
Given this method, a computer program was created providing a command-line 
 interface facilitating human-in-the-loop optimisation.

% ========================================================================================================================================================================================
\subsection{Chemical Background}
The condensation reaction occurring between carboxyl and hydroxyl groups 
 is an esterification reaction (See Eq. \ref{eq:Esterification}).
\begin{equation}
    \begin{split}
        R\textcolor{CarboxylOrange}{COOH}+\textcolor{HydroxylBlue}{H}&\textcolor{HydroxylBlue}{O}R'\rightarrow\\
        &R\textcolor{CarboxylOrange}{CO}\textcolor{HydroxylBlue}{O}R'+\textcolor{CarboxylOrange}{HO}\textcolor{HydroxylBlue}{H}
    \end{split}
    \label{eq:Esterification}
\end{equation}
It is described as a condensation reaction since a small by-product molecule, 
 water, is generated as each ester bond is formed.
As opposed to simple esterification, the desired polyester thermoset, 
 poly(glycerol citrate itaconate), is the product of polyesterification between multi-functional monomers.

Polyesterification is a form of polycondensation reaction which takes place 
 as follows: an initial ester is formed from two difunctional monomers, where 
 one must at least be a diol and the other a diacid (alternatively, each may 
 possess a carboxyl and a hydroxyl group), producing a molecule of water and 
 an ester with two spare functional groups- these groups can subsequently react 
 with more difunctional monomers causing polymerisation into a linear polyester 
 (See Fig.~\ref{fig:SimpleLinearEsterification}).
When monomers with a functionality greater than two are introduced into a 
 polyesterification reaction, then side chains can form along the primary backbone 
 of the growing polyester, this causes the development of a branching polymeric 
 structure (See Fig.~\ref{fig:SimpleReactionOverview}).
When a side chain reaches another large growing polyester molecule and reacts 
 with it a crosslink is said to have developed; when widespread crosslinking occurs 
 generating a 3D network of polyester molecules a thermoset polyester has been created.
Figure~\ref{fig:SimpleReactionOverview} shows how the monomers employed in the synthesis 
 of poly(glycerol citrate itaconate) give rise to this branching thermoset structure.

\begin{figure*}[htb]
    \captionsetup{format=custom,width=0.8\textwidth,skip=0pt}
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{SimpleLinearEsterification}
    \captionof{figure}{\footnotesize{\textbf{SimpleLinearEsterification}Archetypical polyesterification between a diol (propylene glycol) and a diacid (succinic acid) forming a linear polyester. Functional groups participating in the reaction are highlighted: hydroxyl groups blue, and carboxyl groups orange.}}
    \label{fig:SimpleLinearEsterification}
\end{figure*}

Importantly, during the polyesterification process water is generated as the
 only by-product, it is therewith assumed that virtually all mass loss between 
 prepolymer and final cured thermoset would be associated with water evaporation.
Given that each molecule of water produced corresponds to an ester bond formed, 
 mass loss can be said to be a loose proxy for extent of polymerisation within a 
 sample \cite{Trenkel2008}.
This research identifies this as a metric of interest and sought to maximise the 
 mass loss during optimisation of poly(glycerol citrate itaconate) synthesis: 
 increasing the extent of polymerisation and possibly improving the degree of 
 cross-linking and associated strength of these bio-based thermosets.

\subsection{Experimental Method}
Synthesis of the poly(glycerol citrate itaconate) thermoset was achieved 
 via an uncatalysed polyesterification reaction (See Figure \ref{fig:SimpleReactionOverview}).
Acid catalysis was eschewed to avoid corrosion damage to equipment which 
 slowed but failed to halt the polyesterification process \cite{Balachandran2016}.
The monomers glycerol (99+\%), citric acid (99.6\%), and itaconic acid (99+\%) used
 were supplied by \textit{Fisher Scientific International} and manufactured by \textit{Acros Organics}.

\begin{figure*}[htb]
    \captionsetup{format=custom,width=0.8\textwidth,skip=0pt}
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{SimpleReactionOverview}
    \captionof{figure}{\footnotesize{\textbf{SimpleReactionOverview}Polyesterification between glycerol, citric acid, and itaconic acid forming poly(glycerol citrate itaconate). Functional groups participating in the reaction are highlighted: hydroxyl groups blue, and carboxyl groups orange.}}
    \label{fig:SimpleReactionOverview}
\end{figure*}

Thermoset synthesis was two-step: firstly, a homogenous prepolymer resin was 
 prepared by stirring solid monomers (acids) together with liquid monomer (glycerol) 
 in-vitro atop a hotplate and raising the temperature (maximum 140\degree C) 
 until the solids were completely dissolved.
Note, 140\degree C was adopted to accelerate dissolution without encouraging 
 decarboxylation of the citric acid to itaconic acid \cite{Alberts2017}.
Secondly, prepolymer was cured into the desired thermoset by transferral to
 a silicone mould before being exposed to elevated temperatures (90\degree~C) 
 for an extended period (up to two weeks) within a drying oven.

Preliminary experimentation showed that temperatures exceeding 100\degree~C
 caused boiling of byproduct water within samples.
Gaseous water from boiling was entrapped within the curing polyester creating
 vesicles within the final structure, resulting in unpredictable densities of the final material.
Given both the random and uncontrollable nature of samples generated in this 
fashion, drying oven temperatures were held below 90\degree~C to avoid this 
imponderables affect upon the experiments.

As seen in Equation \ref{eq:MassLoss}, determination of mass loss associated 
 with the synthesis of a thermoset sample (\(\Delta m\)) was obtained by measuring 
 the difference between both the initial mass of prepolymer resin (\(m_{p}\)) and the 
 final mass of cured thermoset (\(m_{t}\)).
Measurements were made with a mass balance accurate to 0.01 g ± 0.01 g; since prepolymer 
 samples weighed 3 g and mass loss was confined to between 0.3-0.9 g we assumed
 systematic errors accrued through uncertainty in mass balance readings to be negligible.
\begin{equation}
    \Delta m=m_{p}-m_{t}
    \label{eq:MassLoss}
\end{equation}
Equation \ref{eq:PctMassLoss} shows how sample mass loss (\(\Delta m\)) is normalised 
 into a percentage format (\(\Delta m_{\%}\)), this allowed inter-comparison since 
 individual samples had slightly varying initial prepolymer resin masses (\(m_{p}\)).
\begin{equation}
    \Delta m_{\%}=\frac{\Delta m\times 100}{m_{p}}
    \label{eq:PctMassLoss}
\end{equation}
Residence time within the drying oven for a sample was controlled by periodic 
 measurement of sample mass; mass was plotted against time, an exponential decay 
 curve fitted, and the sample removed from the oven once an arbitrary gradient was reached.

\subsection{Optimisation Framing}
Having selected percentage mass loss within a thermoset sample (\(\Delta m_{\%}\)) 
 as a suitable target variable for optimisation, predictor variables were defined 
 and constrained creating a suitable parameter space describing a full range of 
 prepolymer monomeric compositions.

The polyesterification of monomers to polymer seen in Figure~\ref{fig:SimpleReactionOverview}
 is presented in Equation~\ref{eq:StoichiometricEquation} as an unbalanced stoichiometric 
 equation linking monomeric reagents glycerol (G), itaconic acid (It), and citric acid (Ci)
  with the product polymer poly(glycerol citrate itaconate) (P(GCiIt)) and byproduct water.
Equation~\ref{eq:StoichiometricEquation} introduces two stoichiometric variables representing
 the moles of glycerol (\(n_{G}\)), citric acid (\(n_{Ci}\)) and itaconic acid (\(n_{It}\)), 
 and a variable representing the unknown number of moles of water produced (\(v\)).
\begin{equation}
    \begin{split}
        n_{G}~G+n_{Ci}~Ci&+n_{It}~It\rightarrow\\
        &1~P(GCiIt)+v~H_{2}O
    \end{split}
    \label{eq:StoichiometricEquation}
\end{equation}
The polyesterification reaction can be considered to consist of two possible polymerisation
 reactions between the triol glycerol and either the triacid citric acid or the diacid itaconic acid.
By making a constant of the glycerol stoichiometric variable (\(n_{G}=1\)), 
 Figure~\ref{fig:PolymerisableRange} shows how the two separate reactions can be considered 
 as if they were stoichiometric ranges, where there exist stoichiometric bounds between which polymerisation is possible.

\begin{figure*}[htb]
    \captionsetup{format=custom,width=0.8\textwidth,skip=0pt}
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{PolymerisableRange}
    \captionof{figure}{\footnotesize{\textbf{PolymerisableRange}Simplified chemical representations displaying the polymerisable range of the two different triol-acid combinations encountered during poly(glycerol citrate itaconate) synthesis. In the legend: "G" glycerol, "It" itaconic acid, "Ci" citric acid.}}
    \label{fig:PolymerisableRange}
\end{figure*}

These stoichiometric bounds are used to directly constrain the stoichiometric variables; 
 Equation~\ref{eq:CitricAcidBounds} describes the lower (\(n_{Ci}^{l}\)) and upper (\(n_{Ci}^{u}\)) 
 bounds placed on the citric acid stoichiometric variable, whilst Equation~\ref{eq:ItaconicAcidBounds} 
 shows those for itaconic acids lower (\(n_{It}^{l}\)) and upper (\(n_{It}^{u}\)) bounds.
\begin{equation}
    n_{Ci}^{l}\leq n_{Ci}\leq n_{Ci}^{u}=0.5\leq n_{Ci}\leq 2.0
    \label{eq:CitricAcidBounds}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
    n_{It}^{l}\leq n_{It}\leq n_{It}^{u}=1.0\leq n_{It}\leq 2.0
    \label{eq:ItaconicAcidBounds}
\end{equation}
In addition to the basic polymerisable bounds imposed on the two stoichiometric variables,
 a further constraint must be imposed for a successful optimisation: it must be possible for 
 permutations of prepolymer monomeric mixtures to allow one acid to entirely marginalise the 
 other whilst ensuring that a prepolymer will still polymerise- this constraint ensures that 
 any polymerisable stoichiometric mixture of monomers is possible.
To achieve this, a three-dimensional bounded domain (a.k.a parameter space, \(A\)) was devised
 consisting of three fundamental predictor variables: firstly, a citric acid strength predictor
  variable (\(s_1\)) influencing the citric acid stoichiometric variable.
Secondly, an itaconic acid strength predictor variable (\(s_2\)) influencing the itaconic
 acid stoichiometric variable.
Thirdly, a balancing predictor variable (\(b_1\)) capable of trading off the two acid systems,
 influencing both the citric and itaconic acid stoichiometric variables.
Equation~\ref{eq:ParameterSpaceDimensions} describes the parameter space \(A\) as a multi-dimensional
 real of \(d\) dimensions, where \(d=3\) given our three predictor variables.
\begin{equation}
    A\subseteq \mathbb{R}^{d}
    \label{eq:ParameterSpaceDimensions}
\end{equation}
All three predictor variables took constrained ranges of between 0 and 1 (\(s_1=[0,1],s_2=[0,1],b_1=[0,1]\)),
 and were linked mathematically to the stoichiometric variables 
 (See Equations~\cref{eq:CitricAcidStoichiometricPredictorLink,eq:ItaconicAcidStoichiometricPredictorLink}).
\begin{equation}
    n_{Ci}=(n_{Ci}^{l}+(n_{Ci}^{u}-n_{Ci}^{l})\times s_{1})\times b_{1}
    \label{eq:CitricAcidStoichiometricPredictorLink}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
    n_{It}=(n_{It}^{l}+(n_{It}^{u}-n_{It}^{l})\times s_{2})\times (1-b_{1})
    \label{eq:ItaconicAcidStoichiometricPredictorLink}
\end{equation}
In summation, any random set of predictor variable values drawn from their respective constrained
 ranges of 0 to 1 (e.g. \(s_{1}=0.75,s_{2}=0.25,b_{1}=0.5\)) could be used to calculate a unique 
 permutation of stoichiometric variables (e.g. \(N_{Ci}=0.81,N_{It}=0.63\)) which, on mixing of a
 prepolymer in accordance with Equation~\ref{eq:FinalStoichiometricEquation} and curing, would give
 rise to a unique polymer and associated mass loss (e.g. \(\Delta m_{\%}=30\)).
\begin{equation}
    \begin{split}
        n_{G}~G+0\leq n_{Ci}\leq& n_{Ci}^{u}~Ci+\\
        0\leq n_{It}&\leq n_{It}^{u}~It\rightarrow \\
        1~P&(GCiIt)+v~H_{2}O
    \end{split}
    \label{eq:FinalStoichiometricEquation}
\end{equation}
The sequence of calculations linking abstract predictor variables (\(s_{1},s_{2},b_{1}\)) 
 to the practical stoichiometric variables (\(n_{Ci},n_{It}\)) and onwards to the target variable
 of mass loss (\(\Delta m_{\%}\)), allows the expression of a black-box mapping between 
 predictors and targets.
A black-box mapping is described by Equation~\ref{eq:ParameterSpaceMapping}, where an unknown
 function (\(f\)) maps any point in our parameter space (\(A\)) to a single real number value (\(\mathbb{R}\)).
\begin{equation}
    f:A\rightarrow \mathbb{R}
    \label{eq:ParameterSpaceMapping}
\end{equation}
Equation~\ref{eq:BlackBoxFunction} more explicitly expresses the black-box function
 in terms of the our target variable and predictor variables at a given sample number (\(i\)).
Note that sample number is an integer counter used to track the number of function
 evaluations carried out during a sequential optimisation.
\begin{equation}
    \Delta m_{\%}=f(\mathbf{x})=f([s_{1}^{i},s_{2}^{i},b_{1}^{i}])
    \label{eq:BlackBoxFunction}
\end{equation}
Our optimisation problem is expressed in Equation~\ref{eq:OptimisingBlackBoxFunction}
 as the maximisation of the black-box function, where the global optimum set of predictor variables (\(\mathbf{x}^{*}\)) is sought.
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{x}^{*}=\argmaxA_{\mathbf{x}\in A} f(\mathbf{x})
    \label{eq:OptimisingBlackBoxFunction}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Optimisation Algorithm}
The sequential optimisation algorithm employed during this work is summarised
 in Figure~\ref{fig:OptimisationAlgorithm}.
Cessation of the optimisation is controlled by an if-else clause comparing an 
 initially specified permissable number of function evaluations (a.k.a. sample limit, \(p_{tot}\))
 with the current sample number (\(i\)): when \(i<p_{tot}\) the algorithm continues.
A second if-else statement controls whether a dataset exists from which a Gaussian process
 prior might be built for Bayesian sampling, when available, the algorithm uses the Gaussian
 process to draw a pre-defined number of samples (\(p_{iter}\)) from the parameter space for evaluation.
If the prior-forming dataset is lacking, an initial quasirandom (Sobol) sampling of 
 the parameter space with a pre-defined number of deterministic samples (\(p_{init}\)) is initiated.
Samples drawn from the parameter space are processed into stoichiometric variables
 (\(n_{Ci},n_{It}\)), with which an experimenter can manufacture sample(s) before evaluating 
 the target variables \(\Delta m_{\%}\) value(s) and returning these to the algorithm.

The algorithm seen in Figure~\ref{fig:OptimisationAlgorithm} was implemented in the Python programming
 language, with multiple modules written and united into a single package with the acronym "BO4BT" (version 1.0.0)- 
 Bayesian optimisation for biodegradable thermosets \cite{HojlundDodd2024}.

\begin{center}
    \captionsetup{format=custom,width=0.48\textwidth,skip=0pt}
    \includegraphics[width=0.48\textwidth]{OptimisationAlgorithm}
    \captionof{figure}{\footnotesize{\textbf{OptimisationAlgorithm}} Flow diagram depicting the sequential optimisation algorithm developed.}
    \label{fig:OptimisationAlgorithm}
\end{center}

On calculation of stoichiometric variables during an iteration, the algorithm merges 
 all new sets of predictors variable values into a growing predictor-matrix (\(\mathbf{X}\)),
 shown in Equation~\ref{eq:MatrixOfPredictors}.
\begin{equation}
    \begin{split}
        \mathbf{X}=&[\mathbf{x}^{i=1},\mathbf{x}^{i=2},...]\\
        =&[[s_{1}^{i=1},s_{2}^{i=1},b_{1}^{i=1}],[s_{1}^{i=2},...],...]
    \end{split}
    \label{eq:MatrixOfPredictors}
\end{equation}
On conclusion of an iteration, the algorithm merges all data associated with samples 
 evaluated into an optimisation-governing dataset.
Equation~\ref{eq:OptimisationDataset} presents this dataset (\(\mathbb{D}\)) as a matrix 
 which consists of the predictor-matrix (\(\mathbf{X}\)) and an array of target variable
 values (\(\mathbf{\Delta m_{\%}}\)).
\begin{equation}
    \begin{split}
        \mathbb{D}=&[\mathbf{X},f(\mathbf{X})]\\
        =&[\mathbf{X},\mathbf{\Delta m_{\%}}]\\
        =&[[\mathbf{x}^{i=1},\mathbf{x}^{i=2},...],[\Delta m_{\%}^{i=1},...]]
    \end{split}
    \label{eq:OptimisationDataset}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Sampling Procedures}
During a sequential optimisation two forms of sampling were employed: firstly, a
 deterministic space-filling sampling of a quasirandom nature was used to representatively
 sample the parameter space when initialising the optimisation.
Subequently, a Bayesian sampling technique was used to sample-efficiently guide the
 optimisation's search for the global optimimum \(x^{*}\).
\subsubsection{Initial Quasirandom Sampling}
Optimisation routines were initiated by representatively sampling across the parameter space
 \(A\); achieved using the quasirandom Sobol sampling algorithm to draw a preset number (\(p_{init}\)) 
 of samples from across \(A\).
Sobol sampling was employed as it provides the same or better space-filling properties across \(\mathbb{R}^{1\leq d\leq 40}\) 
 domains as compared with pseudorandom or grid techniques \cite{Garud2017}.
On evaluation of the initial representative set of samples (\([\mathbf{x}^{i=1},...,\mathbf{x}^{i=p_{init}}]\)) for 
 the target variable values (\([\Delta m_{\%}^{i=1},...,\Delta m_{\%}^{i=p_{init}}]\)), an initial 
 prior-forming dataset (\(\mathbb{D}^{i=p_{init}}\)) is produced.
This research employed only two quasirandomly sampled initial prior-forming datasets, facilitating 
 inter-comparison of various Bayesian optimisation routines attempted; the smaller (\(\mathbb{D}^{i=8}\)) 
 and larger (\(\mathbb{D}^{i=16}\)) of these are visualised in Figure~\ref{fig:BayesianPriorsAbbreviated}.

\subsubsection{Sequential Bayesian Sampling}

Sequential sampling used in this research abided by Bayes theorem (see Equation~\ref{eq:Bayesianism}), 
 where a posterior probability \(P(H|E)\) (updated probability of hypothesis H given new evidence \(E\)) 
 is equal to the prior probability (original probability of hypothesis) multiplied by the likelihood \(P(E|H)\) 
 (probability of evidence given hypothesis) divided by the marginal probability \(P(E)\) (probability of evidence).
As new evidence becomes available, the posterior probability is recycled into the equation as a prior alongside 
 the new evidence and updated to a new posterior \cite{Downey2013}.

\begin{equation}
    \overset{\mathrm{posterior}}{P(H|E)}=\frac{\overset{\mathrm{prior}}{P(H)}\times \overset{\mathrm{likelihood}}{P(E|H)}}{\underset{\mathrm{marginal}}{P(E)}}
    \label{eq:Bayesianism}
\end{equation}
Bayesian optimisation uses a Bayesian statistical model called a Gaussian process 
 regression model (a form of Bayesian posterior distribution) derived from Gaussian 
 process regression of a dataset \(\mathbb{D}\) \cite{Rasmussen2005}.
Equation~\ref{eq:GaussianProcess} shows how a gaussian process, 
 \(\mathcal{GP}\),  is a distribution over functions which is completely
 described by both a mean function, \(m\), and a covariance function, \(k\).
 
\begin{equation}
    f(\mathbf{x})\sim \mathcal{GP}(m(\mathbf{x}),k(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{x}'))
    \label{eq:GaussianProcess}
\end{equation}
More intuitively, Equation~\ref{eq:SurrogateFunction} describes the gaussian
 process as a surrogate function \(\hat{f}(\cdot)\) describing the link 
 between a candidate array of predictor values \(\mathbf{x}\) and a tuple
 containing the mean and the variance of a normal distribution over values
 of \(f\) at \(x\).
\begin{equation}
    \hat{f}(\cdot):\mathbf{x}\mapsto [m(\mathbf{x}),k(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{x}')]
    \label{eq:SurrogateFunction}
\end{equation}
As an infinite-dimensional generalisation of multivariate normal distributions
 the gaussian process inherits the properties of a conjugate prior \cite{Deisenroth2020}, 
 allowing the initial posterior (e.g. \(\mathcal{GP}^{i=8}\)) to be recycled
 as a prior alongside new data (e.g. \(\mathbb{D}+[\mathbf{x}^{i=9},\Delta m^{i=9}]\))
 to generate an updated posterior (e.g. \(\mathcal{GP}^{i=9}\)) in accordance
 with Bayes theorem (see \ref{eq:Bayesianism}).
The surrogate model therewith evolves during the course of sequential
 optimisation as the number of iterations, \(i\), increases: corresponding
 to the updating of beliefs when considering new evidence which hallmarks
 all Bayesian techniques.

 employing three simple benchmarks.

 including pseudorandom, quasirandom (Sobol), and grid to disperse the maximum allowable
 number of samples (\(p_{tot}=27\)) across the parameter space \(A\) (See Figure~\ref{fig:Benchmarks}).
