\section{Method}
In optimising the properties of a poly(glycerol citrate itaconate) thermoset, interlinking method was developed spanning both practical experimental technique and mathematical framing.
Given this method, a computer program was created providing a command-line interface facilitating human-in-the-loop optimisation.

\subsection{Chemical Background}
The condensation reaction occurring between carboxyl and hydroxyl groups is an esterification reaction (See Eq. \ref{eq:Esterification}).
\begin{equation}
    \begin{split}
        R\textcolor{CarboxylOrange}{COOH}+\textcolor{HydroxylBlue}{H}&\textcolor{HydroxylBlue}{O}R'\rightarrow\\
        &R\textcolor{CarboxylOrange}{CO}\textcolor{HydroxylBlue}{O}R'+\textcolor{CarboxylOrange}{HO}\textcolor{HydroxylBlue}{H}
    \end{split}
    \label{eq:Esterification}
\end{equation}
It is described as a condensation reaction since a small by-product molecule, water, is generated as each ester bond is formed.
As opposed to simple esterification, the desired polyester thermoset, poly(glycerol citrate itaconate), is the product of polyesterification between multi-functional monomers.

Polyesterification is a form of polycondensation reaction which takes place as follows: an initial ester is formed from two difunctional monomers, where one must at least be a diol and the other a diacid (alternatively, each may possess a carboxyl and a hydroxyl group), producing a molecule of water and an ester with two spare functional groups- these groups can subsequently react with more difunctional monomers causing polymerisation into a linear polyester (See Fig.~\ref{fig:SimpleLinearEsterification}).
When monomers with a functionality greater than two are introduced into a polyesterification reaction, then side chains can form along the primary backbone of the growing polyester, this causes the development of a branching polymeric structure (See Fig.~\ref{fig:SimpleReactionOverview}).
When a side chain reaches another large growing polyester molecule and reacts with it a crosslink is said to have developed; when widespread crosslinking occurs generating a 3D network of polyester molecules a thermoset polyester has been created.
Figure~\ref{fig:SimpleReactionOverview} shows how the monomers employed in the synthesis of poly(glycerol citrate itaconate) give rise to this branching thermoset structure.

\begin{figure*}[htb]
    \captionsetup{format=custom,width=0.8\textwidth,skip=0pt}
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{SimpleLinearEsterification}
    \captionof{figure}{\footnotesize{\textbf{SimpleLinearEsterification}Archetypical polyesterification between a diol (propylene glycol) and a diacid (succinic acid) forming a linear polyester. Functional groups participating in the reaction are highlighted: hydroxyl groups blue, and carboxyl groups orange.}}
    \label{fig:SimpleLinearEsterification}
\end{figure*}

Importantly, during the polyesterification process water is generated as the only by-product, it is therewith assumed that virtually all mass loss between prepolymer and final cured thermoset would be associated with water evaporation.
Given that each molecule of water produced corresponds to an ester bond formed, mass loss can be said to be a loose proxy for extent of polymerisation within a sample \cite{Trenkel2008}.
This research identifies this as a metric of interest and sought to maximise the mass loss during optimisation of poly(glycerol citrate itaconate) synthesis: increasing the extent of polymerisation and possibly improving the degree of cross-linking and associated strength of these bio-based thermosets.

\subsection{Experimental Method}
Synthesis of the poly(glycerol citrate itaconate) thermoset was achieved via an uncatalysed polyesterification reaction (See Figure \ref{fig:SimpleReactionOverview}).
Acid catalysis was eschewed to avoid corrosion damage to equipment which slowed but failed to halt the polyesterification process \cite{Balachandran2016}.
The monomers glycerol (99+\%), citric acid (99.6\%), and itaconic acid (99+\%) used were supplied by \textit{Fisher Scientific International} and manufactured by \textit{Acros Organics}.

\begin{figure*}[htb]
    \captionsetup{format=custom,width=0.8\textwidth,skip=0pt}
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{SimpleReactionOverview}
    \captionof{figure}{\footnotesize{\textbf{SimpleReactionOverview}Polyesterification between glycerol, citric acid, and itaconic acid forming poly(glycerol citrate itaconate). Functional groups participating in the reaction are highlighted: hydroxyl groups blue, and carboxyl groups orange.}}
    \label{fig:SimpleReactionOverview}
\end{figure*}

Thermoset synthesis was two-step: firstly, a homogenous prepolymer resin was prepared by stirring solid monomers (acids) together with liquid monomer (glycerol) in-vitro atop a hotplate and raising the temperature (maximum 140\degree C) until the solids were completely dissolved.
Note, 140\degree C was adopted to accelerate dissolution without encouraging decarboxylation of the citric acid to itaconic acid \cite{Alberts2017}.
Secondly, prepolymer was cured into the desired thermoset by transferral to a silicone mould before being exposed to elevated temperatures (90\degree~C) for an extended period (up to two weeks) within a drying oven.

Preliminary experimentation showed that temperatures exceeding 100\degree~C caused boiling of byproduct water within samples.
Gaseous water from boiling was entrapped within the curing polyester creating vesicles within the final structure, resulting in unpredictable densities of the final material.
Given both the random and uncontrollable nature of samples generated in this fashion, drying oven temperatures were held below 90\degree~C to avoid this imponderables affect upon the experiments.

As seen in Equation \ref{eq:MassLoss}, determination of mass loss associated with the synthesis of a thermoset sample (\(\Delta m\)) was obtained by measuring the difference between both the initial mass of prepolymer resin (\(m_{p}\)) and the final mass of cured thermoset (\(m_{t}\)).
Measurements were made with a mass balance accurate to 0.01 g ± 0.01 g; since prepolymer samples weighed 3 g and mass loss was confined to between 0.3-0.9 g we assumed systematic errors accrued through uncertainty in mass balance readings to be negligible.

\begin{equation}
    \Delta m=m_{p}-m_{t}
    \label{eq:MassLoss}
\end{equation}

Equation \ref{eq:PctMassLoss} shows how sample mass loss (\(\Delta m\)) is normalised into a percentage format (\(\Delta m_{\%}\)), this allowed inter-comparison since individual samples had slightly varying initial prepolymer resin masses (\(m_{p}\)).

\begin{equation}
    \Delta m_{\%}=\frac{\Delta m\times 100}{m_{p}}
    \label{eq:PctMassLoss}
\end{equation}

Residence time within the drying oven for a sample was controlled by periodic measurement of sample mass; mass was plotted against time, an exponential decay curve fitted, and the sample removed from the oven once an arbitrary gradient was reached.

\subsection{Optimisation Framing}
Having selected percentage mass loss within a thermoset sample (\(\Delta m_{\%}\)) as a suitable target variable for optimisation, predictor variables were defined and constrained creating a suitable parameter space describing a full range of prepolymer monomeric compositions.

The polyesterification of monomers to polymer seen in Figure~\ref{fig:SimpleReactionOverview} is presented in Equation~\ref{eq:StoichiometricEquation} as an unbalanced stoichiometric equation linking monomeric reagents glycerol (G), itaconic acid (It), and citric acid (Ci) with the product polymer poly(glycerol citrate itaconate) (P(GCiIt)) and byproduct water.
Equation~\ref{eq:StoichiometricEquation} introduces two stoichiometric variables representing the moles of glycerol (\(N_{G}\)), citric acid (\(N_{Ci}\)) and itaconic acid (\(N_{It}\)), and a variable representing the unknown number of moles of water produced (\(v\)).

\begin{equation}
    \begin{split}
        N_{G}~G+N_{Ci}~Ci&+N_{It}~It\rightarrow\\
        &1~P(GCiIt)+v~H_{2}O
    \end{split}
    \label{eq:StoichiometricEquation}
\end{equation}

The polyesterification reaction can be considered to consist of two possible polymerisation reactions between the triol glycerol and either the triacid citric acid or the diacid itaconic acid.
By making a constant of the glycerol stoichiometric variable (\(N_{G}=1\)), Figure~\ref{fig:PolymerisableRange} shows how the two separate reactions can be considered as if they were stoichiometric ranges, where there exist stoichiometric bounds between which polymerisation is possible.
These stoichiometric bounds are used to directly constrain the stoichiometric variables; Equation~\ref{eq:CitricAcidBounds} describes the lower (\(N_{Ci}^{l}\)) and upper (\(N_{Ci}^{u}\)) bounds placed on the citric acid stoichiometric variable, whilst Equation~\ref{eq:ItaconicAcidBounds} shows those for itaconic acids lower (\(N_{It}^{l}\)) and upper (\(N_{It}^{u}\)) bounds.

\begin{figure*}[htb]
    \captionsetup{format=custom,width=0.8\textwidth,skip=0pt}
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{PolymerisableRange}
    \captionof{figure}{\footnotesize{\textbf{PolymerisableRange}Simplified chemical representations displaying the polymerisable range of the two different triol-acid combinations encountered during poly(glycerol citrate itaconate) synthesis. In the legend: "G" glycerol, "It" itaconic acid, "Ci" citric acid.}}
    \label{fig:PolymerisableRange}
\end{figure*}

\begin{equation}
    N_{Ci}^{l}\leq N_{Ci}\leq N_{Ci}^{u}=0.5\leq N_{Ci}\leq 2.0
    \label{eq:CitricAcidBounds}
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
    N_{It}^{l}\leq N_{It}\leq N_{It}^{u}=1.0\leq N_{It}\leq 2.0
    \label{eq:ItaconicAcidBounds}
\end{equation}

In addition to the basic polymerisable bounds imposed on the two stoichiometric variables, a further constraint must be imposed for a successful optimisation: it must be possible for permutations of prepolymer monomeric mixtures to allow one acid to entirely marginalise the other whilst ensuring that a prepolymer will still polymerise- this constraint ensures that any polymerisable stoichiometric mixture of monomers is possible.
To achieve this, a three-dimensional bounded domain (a.k.a parameter space, \(A\)) was devised consisting of three fundamental predictor variables: firstly, a citric acid strength predictor variable (\(s_1\)) influencing the citric acid stoichiometric variable.
Secondly, an itaconic acid strength predictor variable (\(s_2\)) influencing the itaconic acid stoichiometric variable.
Thirdly, a balancing predictor variable (\(b_1\)) capable of trading off the two acid systems, influencing both the citric and itaconic acid stoichiometric variables.
Equation~\ref{eq:ParameterSpaceDimensions} describes the parameter space \(A\) as a multi-dimensional real of \(d\) dimensions, where \(d=3\) given our three predictor variables.

\begin{equation}
    A\subseteq \mathbb{R}^{d}
    \label{eq:ParameterSpaceDimensions}
\end{equation}

All three predictor variables took constrained ranges of between 0 and 1 (\(s_1=[0,1],s_2=[0,1],b_1=[0,1]\)), and were linked mathematically to the stoichiometric variables (See Equations~\cref{eq:CitricAcidStoichiometricPredictorLink,eq:ItaconicAcidStoichiometricPredictorLink}).

\begin{equation}
    N_{Ci}=(N_{Ci}^{l}+(N_{Ci}^{u}-N_{Ci}^{l})\times s_{1})\times b_{1}
    \label{eq:CitricAcidStoichiometricPredictorLink}
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
    N_{It}=(N_{It}^{l}+(N_{It}^{u}-N_{It}^{l})\times s_{2})\times (1-b_{1})
    \label{eq:ItaconicAcidStoichiometricPredictorLink}
\end{equation}

In summation, any random set of predictor variable values drawn from their respective constrained ranges of 0 to 1 (e.g. \(s_{1}=0.75,s_{2}=0.25,b_{1}=0.5\)) could be used to calculate a unique permutation of stoichiometric variables (e.g. \(N_{Ci}=0.81,N_{It}=0.63\)) which, on mixing of a prepolymer in accordance with Equation~\ref{eq:FinalStoichiometricEquation} and curing, would give rise to a unique polymer and associated mass loss (e.g. \(\Delta m_{\%}=30\)).

\begin{equation}
    \begin{split}
        N_{G}~G+0\leq N_{Ci}\leq& N_{Ci}^{u}~Ci+\\
        0\leq N_{It}&\leq N_{It}^{u}~It\rightarrow \\
        1~P&(GCiIt)+v~H_{2}O
    \end{split}
    \label{eq:FinalStoichiometricEquation}
\end{equation}

The sequence of calculations linking abstract predictor variables (\(s_{1},s_{2},b_{1}\)) to the practical stoichiometric variables (\(N_{Ci},N_{It}\)) and onwards to the target variable of mass loss (\(\Delta m_{\%}\)), allows the expression of a black-box mapping between predictors and targets.
A black-box mapping is described by Equation~\ref{eq:ParameterSpaceMapping}, where an unknown function (\(f\)) maps any point in our parameter space (\(A\)) to a single real number value (\(\mathbb{R}\)).

\begin{equation}
    f:A\rightarrow \mathbb{R}
    \label{eq:ParameterSpaceMapping}
\end{equation}

Equation~\ref{eq:BlackBoxFunction} more explicitly expresses the black-box function in terms of the our target variable and predictor variables at a given sample number (\(i\)).
Note that sample number is an integer counter used to track the number of function evaluations carried out during a sequential optimisation.

\begin{equation}
    \Delta m_{\%}=f(\mathbf{x})=f([s_{1}^{i},s_{2}^{i},b_{1}^{i}])
    \label{eq:BlackBoxFunction}
\end{equation}

Our optimisation problem is expressed in Equation~\ref{eq:OptimisingBlackBoxFunction} as the maximisation of the black-box function, where the global optimum set of predictor variables (\(\mathbf{x}^{*}\)) is sought.

\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{x}^{*}=\argmaxA_{\mathbf{x}\in A} f(\mathbf{x})
    \label{eq:OptimisingBlackBoxFunction}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Sequential Optimisation}
The sequential optimisation algorithm employed during this work is summarised in Figure~\ref{fig:OptimisationAlgorithm}.
Cessation of the optimisation is controlled by an if-else clause comparing an initially specified permissable number of function evaluations (a.k.a. sample limit, \(N_{s}^{tot}\)) with the current sample number (\(i\)): when \(i<N_{s}^{tot}\) the algorithm continues.
A second if-else statement controls whether a dataset exists from which a Gaussian process prior might be built for Bayesian sampling, when available, the algorithm uses the Gaussian process to draw a pre-defined number of samples (\(N_{s}^{iter}\)) from the parameter space for evaluation.
If the prior-forming dataset is lacking, an initial quasirandom sampling of the parameter space with a pre-defined number of deterministic samples (\(N_{s}^{init}\)) is initiated.
Samples drawn from the parameter space are processed into stoichiometric variables (\(N_{Ci},N_{It}\)), with which an experimenter can manufacture sample(s) before evaluating the target variables \(\Delta m_{\%}\) value(s) and returning these to the algorithm.

\begin{center}
    \captionsetup{format=custom,width=0.48\textwidth,skip=0pt}
    \includegraphics[width=0.48\textwidth]{OptimisationAlgorithm}
    \captionof{figure}{\footnotesize{\textbf{OptimisationAlgorithm}} Flow diagram depicting the sequential optimisation algorithm developed.}
    \label{fig:OptimisationAlgorithm}
\end{center}

On calculation of stoichiometric variables during an iteration, the algorithm merges all new sets of predictors variable values into a growing predictor-matrix (\(\mathbf{X}\)), shown in Equation~\ref{eq:MatrixOfPredictors}.

\begin{equation}
    \begin{split}
        \mathbf{X}=&[\mathbf{x}^{i=1},\mathbf{x}^{i=2},...]\\
        =&[[s_{1}^{i=1},s_{2}^{i=1},b_{1}^{i=1}],[s_{1}^{i=2},...],...]
    \end{split}
    \label{eq:MatrixOfPredictors}
\end{equation}

On conclusion of an iteration, the algorithm merges all data associated with samples evaluated into an optimisation-governing dataset.
Equation~\ref{eq:OptimisationDataset} presents this dataset (\(\mathbb{D}\)) as a matrix which consists of the predictor-matrix (\(\mathbf{X}\)) and an array of target variable values (\(\mathbf{\Delta m_{\%}}\)).

\begin{equation}
    \begin{split}
        \mathbb{D}=&[\mathbf{X},f(\mathbf{X})]\\
        =&[\mathbf{X},\mathbf{\Delta m_{\%}}]\\
        =&[[\mathbf{x}^{i=1},\mathbf{x}^{i=2},...],[\Delta m_{\%}^{i=1},...]]
    \end{split}
    \label{eq:OptimisationDataset}
\end{equation}
