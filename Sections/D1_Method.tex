\section{Method}
In optimising the properties of a poly(glycerol citrate itaconate) thermoset, 
 interlinking method was developed spanning both practical experimental
 technique and mathematical framing.
Given this method, various computer programs were written spanning the entire 
 experimental process, primary functionality was the facilitation of
 human-in-the-loop optimisation in a laboratory setting.
Programs were written in the Python programming language and developed into
 modules, whereafter these were united into a single package, ``BO4BT'',
 which has been made available within it's own Github repository alongside
 its dependencies; a .yaml file is also provided which allows an identical
 Conda environment to be setup, allowing a user to run this code
 \cite{HojlundDodd2024}.

\subsection{Chemical Background}
The condensation reaction occurring between carboxyl and hydroxyl groups 
 is an esterification reaction:
\begin{equation}
    \begin{split}
        R\textcolor{CarboxylOrange}{COOH}+\textcolor{HydroxylBlue}{H}&\textcolor{HydroxylBlue}{O}R'\rightarrow\\
        &R\textcolor{CarboxylOrange}{CO}\textcolor{HydroxylBlue}{O}R'+\textcolor{CarboxylOrange}{HO}\textcolor{HydroxylBlue}{H}
    \end{split}
    \label{eq:Esterification}
\end{equation}
As opposed to the simple esterification shown in
 Equation~\ref{eq:Esterification}, the desired polyester thermoset,
 poly(glycerol citrate itaconate), is the product of polyesterification
 between multi-functional monomers.

Polyesterification is a form of polycondensation reaction which takes place 
 as follows: an initial ester is formed from two difunctional monomers,
 where one must at least be a diol and the other a diacid (alternatively,
 each may possess a carboxyl and a hydroxyl group), producing a molecule
 of water and an ester with two spare functional groups- these groups can
 subsequently react with more difunctional monomers causing polymerisation
 into a linear polyester (See Figure~\ref{fig:SimpleLinearEsterification}).
When monomers with a functionality greater than two are introduced into a 
 polyesterification reaction, then side chains can form along the primary
 backbone of the growing polyester, which causes the development of a
 branching polymeric structure (See Figure~\ref{fig:SimpleReactionOverview}).
When a side chain reaches another large growing polyester molecule and
 reacts with it a crosslink is said to have developed, and when widespread
 crosslinking occurs generating a 3D network of polyester molecules a
 thermoset polyester has been created.
Figure~\ref{fig:SimpleReactionOverview} shows how the monomers employed in
 the synthesis of poly(glycerol citrate itaconate) give rise to this
 branching thermoset structure.

\begin{figure*}[htb]

    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{SimpleLinearEsterification}
    \captionof{figure}{
            \footnotesize{
                Archetypical polyesterification between a diol (propylene
                glycol) and a diacid (succinic acid) forming a linear
                polyester.
                Functional groups participating in the reaction are
                highlighted: hydroxyl groups blue, and carboxyl groups
                orange.
                In the products, ester groups are blue and carbonyl groups
                and water are also orange in accordance with provenance.
            }

\end{figure*}

Importantly, during the polyesterification process water is generated as
 the main by-product, and hence it is assumed that virtually all mass loss
 between prepolymer and final cured thermoset would be associated with
 water evaporation.
Given that each molecule of water produced corresponds to an ester bond
 formed, mass loss can be said to be a good proxy for extent of
 polymerisation within a sample \cite{Trenkel2008}.
Thus, this research identifies mass loss as a metric of interest and sought
 to maximise this during optimisation of poly(glycerol citrate itaconate)
 synthesis: increasing the extent of polymerisation and thereby improving
 the degree of cross-linking and associated strength of these bio-based
 thermosets.

\subsection{Experimental Method}
Synthesis of the poly(glycerol citrate itaconate) thermoset was achieved 
 via an uncatalysed polyesterification reaction (See 
 Figure~\ref{fig:SimpleReactionOverview}).
Acid catalysis was avoided to reduce corrosion damage to equipment, this
 ultimately reduced the rate at which this reaction could be carried out
 but did not prevent polycondensation \cite{Balachandran2016}.
The monomers glycerol (99+\%), citric acid (99.6\%), and itaconic acid
 (99+\%) used were supplied by \textit{Fisher Scientific International}
 and manufactured by \textit{Acros Organics}.

\begin{figure*}[htb]
    \captionsetup{format=custom,width=0.8\textwidth,skip=0pt}
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{SimpleReactionOverview}
    \captionof{figure}{
        \footnotesize{
                Polyesterification between glycerol, citric acid, and
                itaconic acid forming poly(glycerol citrate itaconate).
                Functional groups participating in the reaction are
                highlighted: hydroxyl groups blue, and carboxyl groups
                orange.
                In the products, ester groups are blue and carbonyl groups
                and water are orange in accordance with provenance.
            }
        }
    \label{fig:SimpleReactionOverview}
\end{figure*}

Thermoset synthesis was two-step: firstly, a homogenous prepolymer resin was 
 prepared by stirring solid monomers (acids) together with liquid monomer
 (glycerol) atop a hotplate and raising the temperature to a maximum of 
 140~\unit{\degreeCelsius} until the solids were completely dissolved.
Note, 140~\unit{\degreeCelsius} was adopted to accelerate dissolution
 without encouraging decarboxylation of the citric acid to itaconic
 acid \cite{Alberts2017}.
Secondly, prepolymer was cured into the desired thermoset by transferral to
 a silicone mould before being exposed to elevated temperatures
 (90~\unit{\degreeCelsius}) for an extended period (up to two weeks) within
 a drying oven.

Preliminary experimentation showed that temperatures exceeding
 100~\unit{\degreeCelsius}, caused boiling of byproduct water within samples.
Gaseous water from boiling was entrapped within the curing polyester creating
 bubbles within the final structure, resulting in unpredictable densities
 of the final material.
Given the unpredictable nature of samples generated in this 
 fashion, drying oven temperatures were held below 90~\unit{\degreeCelsius}
 to avoid this uncontrollable effect upon the experiments.

As defined by Equation \ref{eq:MassLoss}, determination of mass loss
 associated with the synthesis of a thermoset sample (\(\Delta m\)) was
 obtained by measuring the difference between both the initial mass of
 prepolymer resin (\(m_{p}\)) and the final mass of cured thermoset
 (\(m_{t}\)).
Measurements were made with a mass balance with a readability of
 0.01~\unit{\gram} and repeatability of 0.01~\unit{\gram} (1\(\sigma\)).
Since prepolymer masses weighed 3~\unit{\gram} and subsequently recorded
 mass losses ranged from between 0.3-0.9\unit{\gram}, this mass balance
 was deemed accurate enough for the purposes of this experiment.
\begin{equation}\label{eq:MassLoss}
    \Delta m=m_{p}-m_{t}
\end{equation}
Equation~\ref{eq:PctMassLoss} defines sample mass loss (\(\Delta m\))
 normalised into a percentage format (\(\Delta m_{\%}\)), this allowed
 inter-comparison since individual samples had slightly varying initial
 prepolymer resin masses (\(m_{p}\)).
\begin{equation}\label{eq:PctMassLoss}
    \Delta m_{\%}=\frac{\Delta m\times 100}{m_{p}}
\end{equation}
Residence time within the drying oven for a sample was controlled by
 periodic measurement of sample mass: mass was plotted against time, an
 exponential decay curve fitted, and the sample removed from the oven once
 a pre-determined rate of mass loss was reached (this equated to around
 -0.028\unit{\percent\per\hour} of the initial mass).

\subsection{Optimisation Framing}
Having selected percentage mass loss within a thermoset sample
 (\(\Delta m_{\%}\)) as a suitable target variable for optimisation,
 predictor variables were defined and constrained creating a suitable
 parameter space describing a full range of prepolymer monomeric
 compositions.

The polyesterification of monomers to polymer seen in 
 Figure~\ref{fig:SimpleReactionOverview} is presented in 
 Equation~\ref{eq:StoichiometricEquation} as a parameterised stoichiometric 
 equation linking monomeric reagents glycerol (G), itaconic acid (It), and 
 citric acid (Ci) with the product polymer poly(glycerol citrate itaconate) 
 (P(GCiIt)) and byproduct water.
Equation~\ref{eq:StoichiometricEquation} introduces two stoichiometric
 variables representing the moles of glycerol (\(n_{G}\)), citric acid
 (\(n_{Ci}\)) and itaconic acid (\(n_{It}\)), and a variable representing
 the unknown number of moles of water produced (\(v\)).
\begin{equation}\label{eq:StoichiometricEquation}
    \begin{split}
        n_{G}~G+n_{Ci}~Ci&+n_{It}~It\rightarrow\\
        &1~P(GCiIt)+v~H_{2}O
    \end{split}
\end{equation}
The polyesterification reaction can be considered to consist of two possible 
 polymerisation reactions between the triol glycerol and either the triacid
 citric acid or the diacid itaconic acid.
By setting the glycerol stoichiometric variable to unity (\(n_{G}=1\)), 
 Figure~\ref{fig:PolymerisableRange} shows how the two separate reactions
 can be considered as if they were stoichiometric ranges, where there exist 
 stoichiometric bounds between which polymerisation is possible.

\begin{figure*}[htb]
    \captionsetup{format=custom,width=0.8\textwidth,skip=0pt}
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{PolymerisableRange}
    \captionof{figure}{
        \footnotesize{
            Simplified chemical representations displaying the polymerisable
            range of the two different triol-acid combinations encountered
            during poly(glycerol citrate itaconate) synthesis.
            In the legend: "G" glycerol, "It" itaconic acid, "Ci" citric acid.
        }
    }
    \label{fig:PolymerisableRange}
\end{figure*}

These stoichiometric bounds are used to directly constrain the stoichiometric 
 variables; Equation~\ref{eq:CitricAcidBounds} describes the lower 
 (\(n_{Ci}^{l}\)) and upper (\(n_{Ci}^{u}\)) bounds placed on the citric acid 
 stoichiometric variable, whilst Equation~\ref{eq:ItaconicAcidBounds} 
 shows those for lower (\(n_{It}^{l}\)) and upper (\(n_{It}^{u}\))
 bounds on proportion of itaconic acid.
\begin{equation}\label{eq:CitricAcidBounds}
    n_{Ci}^{l}\leq n_{Ci}\leq n_{Ci}^{u}=0.5\leq n_{Ci}\leq 2.0
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}\label{eq:ItaconicAcidBounds}
    n_{It}^{l}\leq n_{It}\leq n_{It}^{u}=1.0\leq n_{It}\leq 2.0
\end{equation}
In addition to the basic polymerisable bounds imposed on the two
 stoichiometric variables, a further constraint must be imposed for a
 successful optimisation: it must be possible for permutations of
 prepolymer monomeric mixtures to allow one acid to entirely dominate the
 other whilst ensuring that a prepolymer will still polymerise- this
 constraint ensures that any polymerisable stoichiometric mixture of
 monomers is possible.
To achieve this, a three-dimensional bounded domain (a.k.a. parameter
 space, \(A\)) was devised consisting of three fundamental predictor
 variables: firstly, a citric acid strength predictor variable (\(s_1\))
 influencing the citric acid stoichiometric variable.
Secondly, an itaconic acid strength predictor variable (\(s_2\)) influencing
 the itaconic acid stoichiometric variable.
Thirdly, a balancing predictor variable (\(b_1\)) capable of trading off
 the two acid systems, influencing both the citric and itaconic acid
 stoichiometric variables.
\(A\subseteq \mathbb{R}^{d}\) describes the parameter space \(A\) as 
 a multi-dimensional real of \(d\) dimensions, where \(d=3\) given our
 three predictor variables.
All three predictor variables took constrained ranges of between 0 and 1 
 (\(s_1=[0,1],s_2=[0,1],b_1=[0,1]\)), and were linked mathematically to the 
 stoichiometric variables (via equations
 \ref{eq:CitricAcidStoichiometricPredictorLink}
 and \ref{eq:ItaconicAcidStoichiometricPredictorLink}).
\begin{equation}\label{eq:CitricAcidStoichiometricPredictorLink}
    n_{Ci}=(n_{Ci}^{l}+(n_{Ci}^{u}-n_{Ci}^{l})\times s_{1})\times b_{1}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}\label{eq:ItaconicAcidStoichiometricPredictorLink}
    n_{It}=(n_{It}^{l}+(n_{It}^{u}-n_{It}^{l})\times s_{2})\times (1-b_{1})
\end{equation}
In summary, any random set of predictor variable values drawn from their
 respective constrained ranges of 0 to 1
 (e.g. \(s_{1}=0.75,s_{2}=0.25,b_{1}=0.5\)) could be used to calculate a
 unique permutation of stoichiometric variables (e.g. 
 \(N_{Ci}=0.81,N_{It}=0.63\)) which, on mixing of a prepolymer in
 accordance with Equation~\ref{eq:FinalStoichiometricEquation} and curing,
 would give rise to a unique polymer and associated mass loss
 (e.g. \(\Delta m_{\%}=30\)).
\begin{equation}\label{eq:FinalStoichiometricEquation}
    \begin{split}
        n_{G}~G+0\leq n_{Ci}\leq& n_{Ci}^{u}~Ci+\\
        0\leq n_{It}&\leq n_{It}^{u}~It\rightarrow \\
        1~P&(GCiIt)+v~H_{2}O
    \end{split}
\end{equation}
The sequence of calculations linking abstract predictor variables 
 (\(s_{1},s_{2},b_{1}\)) to the practical stoichiometric variables 
 (\(n_{Ci},n_{It}\)) and onwards to the target variable of mass loss 
 (\(\Delta m_{\%}\)), allows the expression of a black-box mapping between 
 predictors and targets.
This black-box mapping is described by \(f:A\rightarrow \mathbb{R}\),
 where an unknown function (\(f\)) maps any point in our parameter space 
 (\(A\)) to a single real number value (\(\mathbb{R}\)).
Equation~\ref{eq:BlackBoxFunction} more explicitly expresses the black-box 
 function in terms of the our target variable and predictor variables at a 
 given sample number (\(i\)).
Note that sample number is an integer counter used to track the number of
 function evaluations carried out during a sequential optimisation.
\begin{equation}\label{eq:BlackBoxFunction}
    \Delta m_{\%}=f(\mathbf{x})=f([s_{1}^{i},s_{2}^{i},b_{1}^{i}])
\end{equation}
Our optimisation problem is thus expressed in 
 Equation~\ref{eq:OptimisingBlackBoxFunction} as the maximisation of the 
 black-box function, where the global optimum set of predictor variables 
 (\(\mathbf{x}^{*}\)) is sought.
\begin{equation}\label{eq:OptimisingBlackBoxFunction}
    \mathbf{x}^{*}=\argmaxA_{\mathbf{x}\in A} f(\mathbf{x})
\end{equation}

\subsection{Optimisation Algorithm}
The sequential optimisation algorithm employed during this work is summarised
 by the flow diagram in Figure~\ref{fig:OptimisationAlgorithm}.
Succesful conclusion of the optimisation is controlled by an if-else clause 
 comparing an initially specified permissable number of function evaluations 
 (a.k.a. sample limit, \(p_{tot}\)) with the current sample number (\(i\)):
 when \(i<p_{tot}\) the algorithm continues.
A second if-else statement controls whether a dataset exists from which a
 Gaussian process prior might be built for Bayesian sampling. 
When available, the algorithm uses the Gaussian process to draw a
 pre-defined number of samples (\(p_{iter}\)) from the parameter space for
 evaluation.
If the prior-forming dataset is lacking, an initial quasirandom (Sobol)
 sampling of the parameter space with a pre-defined number of deterministic
 samples (\(p_{init}\)) is initiated.
Samples drawn from the parameter space are processed into stoichiometric
 variables (\(n_{Ci},n_{It}\)), with which an experimenter can manufacture
 sample(s) before evaluating the target variables \(\Delta m_{\%}\)
 value(s) and returning these to the algorithm.

The algorithm seen in Figure~\ref{fig:OptimisationAlgorithm} was implemented
 in the Python programming language, with multiple modules written and
 united into a single package with the acronym ``BO4BT'' (version 1.0.0)-
 ``Bayesian optimisation for biodegradable thermosets'' \cite{HojlundDodd2024}.

\begin{center}
    \captionsetup{format=custom,width=0.48\textwidth,skip=0pt}
    \includegraphics[width=0.48\textwidth]{OptimisationAlgorithm}
    \captionof{figure}{
        \footnotesize{
            Flow diagram depicting the sequential optimisation algorithm
            developed.
        }
    }
    \label{fig:OptimisationAlgorithm}
\end{center}

On calculation of stoichiometric variables during an iteration, the
 algorithm merges all new sets of predictors variable values into a growing
 predictor-matrix (\(\mathbf{X}\)), shown in Equation~\ref{eq:MatrixOfPredictors}.
\begin{equation}\label{eq:MatrixOfPredictors}
    \begin{split}
        \mathbf{X}=&[\mathbf{x}^{i=1},\mathbf{x}^{i=2},...]\\
        =&[[s_{1}^{i=1},s_{2}^{i=1},b_{1}^{i=1}],[s_{1}^{i=2},...],...]
    \end{split}
\end{equation}
On conclusion of an iteration, the algorithm merges all data associated
 with samples evaluated into an optimisation-governing dataset.
Equation~\ref{eq:OptimisationDataset} presents this dataset (\(\mathbb{D}\))
 as a matrix which consists of the predictor-matrix (\(\mathbf{X}\)) and
 an array of target variable
 values (\(\mathbf{\Delta m_{\%}}\)).
\begin{equation}\label{eq:OptimisationDataset}
    \begin{split}
        \mathbb{D}=&[\mathbf{X},f(\mathbf{X})]\\
        =&[\mathbf{X},\mathbf{\Delta m_{\%}}]\\
        =&[[\mathbf{x}^{i=1},\mathbf{x}^{i=2},...],[\Delta m_{\%}^{i=1},...]]
    \end{split}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Sampling Procedures}
During a sequential optimisation two forms of sampling were employed: 
 firstly, a deterministic space-filling sampling of a quasirandom nature was
 used to representatively sample the parameter space when initialising the
 optimisation.
Subequently, a Bayesian sampling technique was used to sample-efficiently
 guide the optimisation's search for the global optimimum \(x^{*}\).
\subsubsection{Initial Quasirandom Sampling}
Optimisation routines were initiated by representatively sampling across
 the parameter space \(A\); achieved using the quasirandom Sobol sampling
 algorithm to draw a preset number (\(p_{init}\)) of samples from
 across \(A\).
Sobol sampling was employed as it provides the same or better
 space-filling properties across \(\mathbb{R}^{1\leq d\leq 40}\) domains
 as compared with pseudorandom or grid techniques \cite{Garud2017}.
On evaluation of the initial representative set of samples
 (\([\mathbf{x}^{i=1},...,\mathbf{x}^{i=p_{init}}]\)) for the target
 variable values (\([\Delta m_{\%}^{i=1},...,\Delta m_{\%}^{i=p_{init}}]\)),
 an initial prior-forming dataset (\(\mathbb{D}^{i=p_{init}}\)) is produced.
In practice, only one quasirandomly sampled initial prior-forming dataset 
 (\(\mathbb{D}^{i=8}\)) was generated and used (visualised in Figure 
 \ref{fig:BayesianPriorsAbbreviated}); this allowed a fair
 inter-comparison of the two Bayesian optimisation routines attempted.

\subsubsection{Sequential Bayesian Sampling}
% Pedagogical Information which could be of use in the thesis introductory
% material:

% Sequential sampling used in this research used Bayes theorem
%  (see Equation~\ref{eq:Bayesianism}), where a posterior probability \(P(H|E)\)
%  (updated probability of hypothesis H given new evidence \(E\)) is equal to the 
%  prior probability (original probability of hypothesis) multiplied by the 
%  likelihood \(P(E|H)\) (probability of evidence given hypothesis) divided by the 
%  marginal probability \(P(E)\) (probability of evidence).
% As new evidence becomes available, the posterior probability is recycled into 
%  the equation as an updated prior alongside the new evidence and used to produce
%  a new posterior \cite{Downey2013}.
% \begin{equation}\label{eq:Bayesianism}
%     \overset{\mathrm{posterior}}{P(H|E)}=\frac{\overset{\mathrm{prior}}{P(H)}\times \overset{\mathrm{likelihood}}{P(E|H)}}{\underset{\mathrm{marginal}}{P(E)}}
% \end{equation}
% Bayesian optimisation uses a Bayesian statistical model called a Gaussian process 
%  regression model (a form of Bayesian posterior distribution) derived from Gaussian 
%  process regression of a dataset \(\mathbb{D}\) \cite{Rasmussen2005}.
% Equation~\ref{eq:GaussianProcess} shows how a gaussian process, 
%  \(\mathcal{GP}\),  is a distribution over functions which is completely
%  described by both a mean function, \(m\), and a covariance function, \(K\).
 
% \begin{equation}\label{eq:GaussianProcess}
%     f(\mathbf{x})\sim \mathcal{GP}(m(\mathbf{x}),K(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{x}'))
% \end{equation}

% More intuitively, Equation~\ref{eq:SurrogateFunction} describes the gaussian
%  process as a surrogate function \(\hat{f}(\cdot)\) describing the link 
%  between a candidate array of predictor values \(\mathbf{x}\) and a tuple
%  containing the mean and the variance of a normal distribution over values
%  of \(f\) at \(x\).

% \begin{equation}\label{eq:SurrogateFunction}
%     \hat{f}(\cdot):\mathbf{x}\mapsto [m(\mathbf{x}),K(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{x}')]
% \end{equation}

Sequential sampling by means of Bayesian optimisation was undertaken using
 a Gaussian process regression model, which was derived from the regression
 of an optimisation dataset \(\mathbb{D}\).
Equation~\ref{eq:GaussianProcess} shows how the Gaussian process,
 \(\mathcal{GP}\), is a distribution over functions which is completely
 described by both a mean function, \(m\), and a covariance function, \(K\).
\begin{equation}\label{eq:GaussianProcess}
    f(\mathbf{x})\sim \mathcal{GP}(m(\mathbf{x}),K(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{x}'))
\end{equation}

As an infinite-dimensional generalisation of multivariate normal
 distributions the Gaussian process inherits the properties of a conjugate
 prior \cite{Deisenroth2020}, allowing the initial posterior
 (e.g. \(\mathcal{GP}^{i=8}\)) to be recycled as a prior alongside new
 data (e.g. \(\mathbb{D}+[\mathbf{x}^{i=9},\Delta m^{i=9}]\)) to generate
 an updated posterior (e.g. \(\mathcal{GP}^{i=9}\)) in accordance
 with Bayes theorem.
The surrogate model therewith evolves during the course of sequential
 optimisation as the number of iterations, \(i\), increases: corresponding
 to the updating of beliefs when considering new evidence.

During fitting of the Gaussian process a covariance function is used to
 encode beliefs regarding the families of functions likely to fit the
 data observed; this work made use of the Mat\'ern family.
When using the Mat\'ern covariance function a hyperparameter controlling
 smoothness of functions drawn from the Gaussian process was set
 (\(\nu=2.5\))- this encouraged the generation of relatively smooth
 functions, which was deemed acceptable given that preliminary sampling of
 the parameter space indicated a similar topography.

Subsequent of posterior genesis, a simple one-step lookahead sampling
 policy was used to select a suitable next point \(\mathbf{x}\) within the
 parameter space \(A\) to sample.
This research focused upon policies that made use of the `simple reward'
 utility function (see Equation \ref{eq:UtilityFunction}) which takes the
 current dataset \(\mathbb{D}\), and from the set of sample coordinates
 evaluated so far \(X\) returns the predicted mean value \(\Delta m_{\%}\)
 of the point \(\mathbf{x}\) exhibiting the highest predicted mean
 (\(\mathrm{max}\ m(X)\)) according to the current posterior \(\mathcal{GP}\)
 \cite{Garnett2023}.

\begin{equation}\label{eq:UtilityFunction}
    u(\mathbb{D})=\mathrm{max}\ m(X)
\end{equation}

In adopting this utility function, two different sampling policies were
 developed and investigated: the first was predicated on the `probability of
 improvement' acquisition function (defined in
 Equation~\ref{eq:ProbabilityOfImprovement}) where any given putative
 \(\mathbf{x}\) is scored by its probability that it will produce an
 updated dataset \(\mathbb{D}^{\prime}\) that will generate a higher
 evaluation from the utility function than an absolute threshold \(\tau\).
The probability of a sample exceeding the current best is deduced using the
 current posterior \(\mathcal{GP}\)'s mean (\(m(\textbf{x})\))and standard
 deviation (\(\sqrt{K(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{x})}\)) measured at that sample
 position.
Note, this work made the absolute threshold equal to an evaluation of the
 utility function using the current dataset (\(\tau=u(\mathbb{D})\)),
 eschewing the added complication of deciding upon a marginal threshold
 parameter \(\varepsilon\) which could have been used
 (\(\tau=u(\mathbb{D})+\varepsilon\)) to offset any overly exploitative
 behaviour induced by rewarding any improvement no matter the scale.

\footnotesize
\begin{equation}\label{eq:ProbabilityOfImprovement}
    \begin{split}
        \alpha_{PI}(\mathbf{x})=&Pr(u(\mathbb{D}^{\prime})>\tau)\\
        =&\int_{\tau}^{\infty}\mathcal{N}(m(\mathbf{x}),\sqrt{K(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{x})})dm(\mathbf{x})\\
        =&1-\Phi((\tau-m(\mathbf{x}))/\sqrt{K(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{x})})
    \end{split}
\end{equation}
\normalsize

The second policy made use of the `expected improvement' acquisition
 function (defined in Equation \ref{eq:ExpectedImprovement}), which
 incorporates an expectation of improvement value
 (\(u(\mathbb{D}^{\prime})-u(\mathbb{D})\)); this counteracts overtly
 exploitative behaviour by rewarding both the scale and probability of an
 improvement above the threshold \(\tau\) \cite{Teh2020}.

\footnotesize
\begin{equation}\label{eq:ExpectedImprovement}
    \begin{split}
        &\alpha_{EI}(\mathbf{x})=\int_{\tau}^{\infty}c\ \mathcal{N}(m(\mathbf{x}),\sqrt{K(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{x})})dm(x)\\
        &\textnormal{where}\ c=u(\mathbb{D}^{\prime})-u(\mathbb{D})\\
    \end{split}
\end{equation}
\normalsize

Equation \ref{eq:AcquisitionFunctionOptimisation} shows how the next point
 to be sampled sampled \(\mathbf{x}^{+}\) is found by trivially optimising
 the cheap acquisition function (many putative samples \(\textbf{x}\) may be
 evaluated) to find the global optimum within the bounds of the parameter 
 space \(A\) \cite{Teh2020}.

\begin{equation}\label{eq:AcquisitionFunctionOptimisation}
    \mathbf{x}^{+}=\argmaxA_{\mathbf{x}\in A}\alpha(\mathbf{x})
\end{equation}

Gaussian processes and acquisition functions were implemented using two
 open-source packages: scikit-optimise ("skopt", v0.9.0) \cite{Kernc2021}
 and Ax ("ax-platform", v0.4.3) \cite{Facebook2024}.

 number of samples (\(p_{tot}=27\)) across the parameter space \(A\) (See Figure~\ref{fig:Benchmarks}).

\subsection{Emulation of Physical System}

An emulation of the physical system investigated facilitated statistical
 analyses of the various sampling techniques' efficacies in discovery of
 the experimental global optimum.
This emulation was developed by firstly concatenating the final datasets 
 \(\mathbb{D}\) generated from the three benchmark and two sequential 
 techniques deployed- this master dataset which represented all the
 knowledge of the objective function's topography within the parameter space
 investigated.
Subsequently, a Gaussian process \(\mathcal{GP}\) was trained on this
 master dataset using the parameterisations described earlier-
 the resultant mean function \(m(\mathbf{x})\) then being used as an
 inexpensive surrogate function of the latent objective function (i.e. the
 emulator).
Note, that some data obtained outside the sample limit was included in this
 master dataset- specifically, the final samples generated by the Bayesian 
 sequential technique using the `probability of improvement' acquisition
 function; these were remarkably high, and represented the experimental
 global optimum discovered during the course of this work.
Care was taken to ensure the trained \(\mathcal{GP}\) faithfully
 replicated this region of optimality within the parameter space as well
 as the general trends of the objective function.
