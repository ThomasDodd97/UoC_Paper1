\section{Results}
This work produced two sets of results: firstly, those derived from optimisation
 conducted via sampling directly from the latent objective function described by
 the physical system.
Secondly, those stemming from the optimisation of a surrogate function trained to
 emulate the latent objective function and the associated physical system.
The latter set of results were generated to provide statistical support for
 the former.

% ========================================================================================================================================================================================
\subsection{Physical System Optimisation}
Two Bayesian sequential optimisation experiments were run, each with the same
 sample limit (\(p_{tot}=27\)), number of samples drawn per Bayesian 
 iteration (\(p_{iter}=3\)), and prior-forming dataset of 8 quasirandomly
 sampled targets (See Figure \ref{fig:BayesianPriorsAbbreviated}).
Differing between these two methods was the acquisition function employed: the
 first using the 'probability of improvement' whilst the second used the
 'expected improvement' function.
Figure \ref{fig:ResultOverview} shows the optimisation progression of these two
 sequential techniques when compared with the one-shot deterministic methods;
 mass losses plotted on the y-axis are the best values uncovered given the
 samples evaluated so far, \(\Delta m_{\%}^{\circ}\).
A statistical overview is presented in Table \ref{tab:BayesianSequentialsBestValues}
 which concerns the 8 quasirandom and 16 sequential samples for each of the two
 Bayesian techniques.
Note, that given the sequential samples available (\(p_{tot}-p_{init}=19\))
 and the samples per iteration (\(p_{iter}=3\)), an extra iteration of sequential
 sampling was run, which breached the sample limit- these extra samples are
 included in Figure \ref{fig:ResultOverview}, but are not considered in the
 preparation of Table \ref{tab:BayesianSequentialsBestValues}.

By comparing Tables \ref{tab:BayesianSequentialsBestValues} and \ref{tab:BenchmarkBestValues}
 it is apparent that both sequential routines outperformed the one-shot techniques
 in terms of best sample discovered.
Figure \ref{fig:ResultOverview} shows that the sequential Bayesian technique employing
 the 'expected improvement' acquisition function discovered a point exceeding the
 strongest one-shot technique in almost half the allowable samples (63\% \(p_{tot}\)).
In contrast, the 'probability of improvement' acquisition function only managed to
 discover a point surpassing the grid sampling benchmark at the sample limit itself
 (100\% \(p_{tot}\))- this was expected given its bias towards parameter space
 exploration relative to the 'expected improvement' acquisition function's more
 exploitative tendencies.
Further evidence of the 'probability of improvement' acquisition functions exploratory
 bias is seen in the Table \ref{tab:BayesianSequentialsBestValues}, where the samples
 generated by this sequential routine obtain a markedly lower mean than that of those
 drawn by the 'expected improvement' acquisition function.

Notably, the Bayesian sequential technique employing the 'probability of improvement'
 acquisition function discovered samples, outside of the sample limit (See Figure 
 \ref{fig:ResultOverview}), attaining the highest mass losses of any sample characterised
 during this work (\(\Delta m_{\%}^{\circ}=20.77\%\)).

\subsection{Emulated System Optimisation}

 sequential sampling techniques was infeasible. This precluded statistically
 proving the utility of Bayesian optimisation in this field within reasonable
 timeframes.

 experiments during this work a master dataset was generated, this was used 
 to develop a surrogate function closely emulating the latent objective function 
 of the physical system.
