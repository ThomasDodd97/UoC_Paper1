\section{Results}
This work produced two sets of results: firstly, those derived from
 optimisation conducted via sampling directly from the latent objective
 function described by the physical system.
Secondly, those stemming from the optimisation of a surrogate function
 trained to emulate the latent objective function and the associated
 physical system.
The latter set of results were generated to provide statistical support for
 the former.

% ========================================================================================================================================================================================
\subsection{Physical System Optimisation}
Two Bayesian sequential optimisation experiments were run, each with the same
 sample limit (\(p_{tot}=27\)), number of samples drawn per Bayesian 
 iteration (\(p_{iter}=3\)), and prior-forming dataset of 8 quasirandomly
 sampled targets (See Figure \ref{fig:BayesianPriorsAbbreviated}).
Differing between these two methods was the acquisition function employed: the
 first using the 'probability of improvement' whilst the second used the
 'expected improvement' function.
Figure \ref{fig:ResultOverview} shows the optimisation progression of these 
 two sequential techniques when compared with the one-shot deterministic 
 methods; mass losses plotted on the y-axis are the best values uncovered 
 given the samples evaluated so far, \(\Delta m_{\%}^{\circ}\).
A statistical overview is presented in Table 
 \ref{tab:BayesianSequentialsBestValues} which concerns the 8 quasirandom 
 and 16 sequential samples for each of the two Bayesian techniques.
Note, that given the sequential samples available (\(p_{tot}-p_{init}=19\))
 and the samples per iteration (\(p_{iter}=3\)), an extra iteration of 
 sequential sampling was run, which breached the sample limit- these extra
 samples are included in Figure \ref{fig:ResultOverview}, but are not
 considered in the preparation of Table 
 \ref{tab:BayesianSequentialsBestValues}.

By comparing Tables \ref{tab:BayesianSequentialsBestValues} and 
 \ref{tab:BenchmarkBestValues} it is apparent that both sequential routines
 outperformed the one-shot techniques in terms of best sample discovered.
Figure \ref{fig:ResultOverview} shows that the sequential Bayesian technique 
 employing the 'expected improvement' acquisition function discovered a 
 point exceeding the strongest one-shot technique in almost half the 
 allowable samples (63\% \(p_{tot}\)).
In contrast, the 'probability of improvement' acquisition function only 
 managed to discover a point surpassing the grid sampling benchmark at the
 sample limit itself (100\% \(p_{tot}\))- this hinted at the 'probability
 of improvement' acquisition function exhibiting more exploratory
 behaviour than the 'expected improvement' acquisition function.
Further evidence of the 'probability of improvement' acquisition functions
 exploratory bias is seen in Table \ref{tab:BayesianSequentialsBestValues},
 where the samples generated by this sequential routine obtain a markedly
 lower mean than that of those drawn by the 'expected improvement'
 acquisition function; anecdotally, on reviewing the placement of samples 
 in 3D space it was noted that these were generally placed in exploratory
 locations within the parameter space.
Interestingly, the evidence collated above allayed fears that the
 'probability of improvement' acquisition function would exhibit overtly
 exploitative behaviour due to the lack of a marginal threshold parameter
 \(\varepsilon\) being manually tuned.

Notably, the Bayesian sequential technique employing the 'probability of
 improvement' acquisition function discovered samples attaining the highest

 (\(\Delta m_{\%}^{\circ}=20.77\%\)), albeit outside the sample limit
 (See Figure \ref{fig:ResultOverview}).

\subsection{Emulated System Optimisation}
Given the temporal expense of sampling the objective function, repetition of 
 sequential sampling techniques was infeasible. This precluded statistically
 proving the utility of Bayesian optimisation in this field within reasonable
 timeframes.
By developing a cheap surrogate function capable of emulating the physical
 system, a much larger number of function evaluations could be carried out
 computationally, facilitating statistical analysis of sampling methods'
 efficacy.
By despatching 1,000 simulations of each of the two random benchmark one-shot
 techniques and the two Bayesian sequential techniques, and returning the
 best target variable values \(\Delta m_{\%}^{\circ}\) discovered by each
 of these, basic statistical conclusions could be drawn.
Note that each simulation of the sequential techniques was initiated 
 with a separate quasirandomly drawn prior of 8 samples and that the grid
 sampler was precluded since its outcomes were not affected by chance.

\begin{figure*}[htb]
    \captionsetup{format=custom,width=0.8\textwidth,skip=0pt}
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{SimulationResultOverview.png}
    \captionof{figure}{\footnotesize{\textbf{SimulationResultOverview} Swarm plot showing the best function evaluations derived from each of 1,000 simulations run for each sampling technique predicated on initial random sampling.}}
    \label{fig:SimulationResultOverview}
\end{figure*}

Figure \ref{fig:SimulationResultOverview} presents a swarmplot showing
 dispersion of outcomes for the 4,000 simulations across the four sampling
 techniques studied.
On comparison of the mean \(\Delta m_{\%}^{\circ}\) values discovered by the
 various sampling techniques, sequential Bayesian techniques were shown to
 generally perform better (PI=14.6\%, EI=15.4\% for 
 \(\Delta m_{\%}^{\circ}\)) than one-shot techniques (PR=14.0\%, 
 QR=14.1\% for \(\Delta m_{\%}^{\circ}\)).
The Bayesian sequential technique adopting the 'expected improvement'
 acquisition function in its sampling policy not only outperformed the
 'probability of improvement' technique in terms of mean value attained,
 but also in number of simulations obtaining \(\Delta m_{\%}^{\circ}\)
 values which resided within the small region of global optimality.
This region was characterised as containing function evaluations exceeding
 18.5\%, and this simple threshold was therewith used to calculate the 
 number of simulations attaining such globally optimum values
 (\(\Delta m_{\%}^{\circ}>18.5\)) for each of the sampling techniques
 (PI=11, EI=53 simulations where \(\Delta m_{\%}^{\circ}>18.5\)).
Notably, neither of the one-shot techniques was able to uncover the
 globally optimum region (PR=0, QR=0 simulations where 
 \(\Delta m_{\%}^{\circ}>18.5\)).
Given the above, and a (particularly) tentative assumption that the final 
 emulation was an accurate description of the underlying objective function,
 we could venture that the probability of having discovered the experimental
 global optimum using the 'expected improvement' and 'probability of 
 improvement' techniques was 5.3\% and 1.1\% respectively.
This would make the practical reality of having discovered these points
 remote but most definitely not impossible; and, importantly, the
 probabilities are a marked improvement above the 0.0\% exhibited by the
 one-shot techniques.
